Homewood

Food Desert

Once a thriving commercial corridor with multiple grocery stores, Homewood has spent decades with zero full-service supermarkets. The neighborhood, technically three sub-neighborhoods (Homewood North, South, West), saw 2 in 5 housing units vacant by recent census estimates. Rising Tide Partners' acquisition of 93 Hamilton Avenue properties marks the most significant community-led revitalization push in decades.

East Hills

Food Desert

Constructed in 1969 as a self-sufficient planned community, East Hills declined sharply in the 1980s–90s as Pittsburgh's economy collapsed. The neighborhood has a 19% real estate vacancy rate, higher than 86% of all U.S. neighborhoods, alongside one of the highest childhood poverty rates in America (75.2% of children below the federal poverty line). Nearest major grocer is a Giant Eagle on Frankstown Road, a car-dependent trip for most residents.

Larimer

Food Desert

Pittsburgh's former "Little Italy," Larimer sits directly adjacent to the booming Bakery Square (home to Google's Pittsburgh offices) yet remains one of the city's most food-insecure neighborhoods. With a 26.6–28% housing vacancy rate, nearly a quarter of all housing sits empty. A $30M HUD Choice Neighborhood grant (2014) funded the Larimer Vision Plan, resulting in Liberty Green Park, a 3-acre green infrastructure park built on formerly vacant land, and new affordable housing construction.

Liberty Green Park completed on former vacant lots — model for vacancy-to-green-space conversion

Knoxville

Green Space Gap

Part of Pittsburgh's Hilltop community, Knoxville sits on steep terrain that limits flat developable land and accessible parkspace. Named alongside Allentown, Arlington, and Homewood as one of Pittsburgh's most food-insecure neighborhoods by Just Harvest. The Hilltop Alliance completed a 2021 property redevelopment report specifically for Knoxville and created the Knoxville Incline Greenway from former vacant lots, demonstrating how vacancy can become green infrastructure in this topographically constrained neighborhood.

Wilkinsburg

Housing Crisis

Adjacent to Pittsburgh's eastern edge, Wilkinsburg Borough has one of the most severe vacancy situations in Allegheny County. Once home to 31,000 residents (1950), the borough now houses approximately 11,334–14,000 people, a population loss of more than half. Nearly 20% of all buildings, over 1,000 parcels, are vacant or derelict, alongside 600+ empty lots. The homeownership rate (32%) is less than half the metro average. Community gardens have begun reclaiming demolished sites as ecological habitat and food production space.

Arlington

Food Desert

One of 14 neighborhoods that Just Harvest prioritizes for food access intervention, Arlington sits on the Hilltop with significant topographic barriers between residents and groceries. The combination of steep terrain, limited transit, and high rates of car-free households creates extreme food access difficulty. The Pittsburgh Post-Gazette documented a resident walking to a dollar store for white milk and being told only chocolate was available, a vivid illustration of daily food desert reality. The Hilltop Urban Farm (107 acres, 23 in active production) is a key asset nearby.

Spring Garden

Green Space Gap

A thin, crescent-shaped neighborhood tucked into a steep valley on Pittsburgh's North Side, Spring Garden is described by its own residents as having "not a great deal of park space within walking distance." Between 1974 and 2010, vacancy spiked from 5% to 25% as the population fell from ~2,000 to ~800. The valley geography creates natural isolation,a condition urban theorists link to entrenched blight. No parks exist within the neighborhood itself; nearest green spaces require hill climbing or car access. Grocery options are similarly limited to a nearby Giant Eagle that most residents must drive to reach.

Oakland

Food Desert

Home to the University of Pittsburgh and Carnegie Mellon University, Oakland presents a paradox: a dense, educated, high-foot-traffic neighborhood with no full-service grocery store. The Giant Eagle on Centre Avenue closed in the 2000s and was never replaced. The closest supermarket is over a mile away in the South Side. Oakland residents are part of the 50% of Pittsburghers living in food deserts (City of Pittsburgh, 2021). The current Oakland Plan includes transportation infrastructure and affordable housing, but no grocery store. More than 100 restaurants serve the area, but restaurants don't meet the food needs of low-income residents or families.

Oakland Plan approved — transportation + housing investments, but no grocery store included as of 2023
